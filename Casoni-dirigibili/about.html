Origins of the Archive

In the years following the First World War, many military departments and industrial companies were forced to redefine their purpose. Structures created for wartime defense were gradually redirected toward research, documentation, and technical development. Among them was the AAA — Anti-Aircraft Artillery, originally established as a military unit during the conflict.

By the mid-1920s, the role of the AAA had begun to change. No longer required to operate in the same military capacity, the department was progressively converted into a technical section dedicated to the analysis of aerial risk. Its attention shifted from the defense of the skies to the study of flight itself: its failures, its accidents, and the technical conditions that made modern air navigation both possible and dangerous.

It was within this transformation that the first materials of what would later become the Adler’s Archive began to be collected.

Friedrich Adler

Friedrich Adler had served within the AAA during the war, where his work involved observation, reporting, and the technical interpretation of aerial threats. After the conflict, his experience made him a suitable figure for a new and more analytical assignment.

Rather than being transferred to an active military post, Adler was placed within the emerging field of aeronautical risk research. His task was not to design airships, nor to promote their use, but to observe their failures with precision. He was asked to collect evidence, compare incidents, and identify recurring patterns in accidents involving large airships, particularly those intended for long-distance and transoceanic navigation.

The official beginning of Adler’s new assignment is marked by a short reply note dated September 15th, 1926. The document, preserved among the earliest papers of the archive, confirms both the transfer and the nature of his future work.

“Reply Note to the Department — September 15th, 1926

I acknowledge the transfer ordered following the conclusion of the conflict and understand its operational reasons. After my service with the AAA — Anti-Aircraft Artillery, I will undertake the new assignment with the same discipline and diligence.

I will devote particular attention to the study of transoceanic airship navigation, with the task of observing, documenting, and analyzing risks, technical anomalies, weather conditions, and testimonies related to airship incidents. I believe that the careful collection of such material may contribute to the future safety of this form of navigation.

It is my intention to continue offering the best possible service to the AAA.

Friedrich Adler”

Adler approached this task with the discipline of a military officer and the caution of an archivist. His notes rarely sought dramatic effect. Instead, they focused on structure, fire, weather, crew response, mechanical weakness, and the sequence of collapse. This note is now considered the founding document of the Adler’s Archive.

The Adler Method

Between 1926 and 1937, Adler collected and organized a vast number of materials related to airship accidents across Europe, Russia, the United States, and South America. His archive included photographs of wreckage, newspaper reports, technical drawings, scale models, eyewitness testimonies, internal memoranda, meteorological observations, and comparative notes.

The archive does not appear to have been created for public circulation. Its original function was technical and internal: to support the study of repeated failures in airship navigation. Adler was particularly interested in the relationship between structural damage and combustion, the behavior of hydrogen during impact and the recurring difficulties of landing or mooring large airships under unstable conditions.

His classification method was unusually precise for its time. Each case was organized according to a set of recurring categories: country, year, probable cause, number of victims, visible structural damage, fire development, weather conditions, and available technical documentation.

This method allowed different materials to be read together. A photograph, a newspaper article, and a technical drawing could belong to the same incident, but they could also be compared with other cases sharing similar characteristics. In fact, many of Adler’s notes compare one accident with another, making the archive not merely a collection of accidents, but a system for interpreting them.

For this reason, the archive often moves between different types of evidence. Images of burning airships are followed by structural diagrams. Newspaper headlines are placed beside technical sketches. Scale models are used to test possible explanations for balance, lift, and collapse.

Decline and Closure of the AAA

As the use of airships gradually declined, the work of the AAA’s technical section also lost its original urgency. The disasters of the 1930s, together with the rise of faster and more reliable aircraft, weakened public confidence in large dirigibles and reduced institutional support for their development.

Following a broader reorganization of technical departments, the activity of the AAA was eventually discontinued. Its operational role had already disappeared, and its research function was absorbed into other aeronautical offices.

The materials collected by Adler, however, were not destroyed. Recognizing their technical and historical value, the remaining archive was transferred to the Archiv für Luftfahrttechnik und Risikoforschung, an institution dedicated to the preservation of documents related to flight safety, technical research, and the memory of European aeronautics.

The Preservation of the Adler’s Archive

Among the collections preserved by the Archiv für Luftfahrttechnik und Risikoforschung, the Adler’s Archive is now considered one of the richest and most complete surviving bodies of material on the history of airship incidents.

Its importance lies not only in the quantity of documents, but in the way they were assembled. The archive preserves the logic of its creator: a careful attempt to understand failure through comparison.

For this reason, the Adler’s Archive occupies a unique position between technical documentation and historical memory. It records the ambition of airship navigation, but also the fragility of the systems that supported it.

The 2026 Publication

In 2026, one hundred years after the archive was first established, we decided to publish a digitized selection of the Adler’s Archive.

Our aim is to make accessible a collection originally created for internal technical analysis and now recognized as an important historical document on safety, failure, and memory in airship navigation.

By publishing this selection, we hope to return visibility to a body of research that remained hidden for decades within technical collections. What began as an internal study of aerial risk has become a record of a vanished era of flight, marked by invention, catastrophe, and the persistent attempt to learn from disaster.
